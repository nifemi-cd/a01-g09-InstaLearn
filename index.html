Vision

Vision Statement

InstaLearn is a quiz and flashcard application designed to make studying easier, more convenient, and more fun for high school, college, and university students pursuing their education.

Intended Users

Students often struggle with creating flashcards, generating practice questions, and identifying which material requires more focus. These tasks take time away from actual studying. In addition, students must balance their time effectively while avoiding procrastination and staying motivated, especially in difficult or less interesting courses.

Features

Study Frequency Tracker

Users can visually track how often they have logged in to study over time, helping them understand their consistency and overall study habits.

Study Set Sharing

Users can upload and share study sets, allowing others to access and learn from shared materials.

Incorrect Answer Tracker

Incorrectly answered questions are highlighted for review, helping users focus on weaker areas and improve performance.

Study Notification Reminders

Users can set custom study reminders, encouraging better time management and consistent study habits.

Milestone Rewards

Users earn rewards for correct answers, consistent studying, and app usage, making learning more engaging and motivating.

Quiz Mode

Users can create and play customizable multiple-choice quizzes for efficient and interactive studying.

Flashcard Mode

Users can create and review digital flashcards, making it easy to study and retain key information.

Study Sets Management

Users can modify and manage existing study sets, including quizzes and flashcards, for flexible learning.

User Profiles

A dedicated space for users to view their study statistics, track progress, and monitor achievements, ensuring personalized learning experiences.

Development Postmortem

A reflection on our process, improvements, and lessons learned across three iterations.

The Weakness — Branching & Merging

Our biggest failure across iterations one and two was letting feature branches diverge for too long before merging. In iteration two, this culminated in a catastrophic merge conflict across ten files — hours lost, bugs introduced, and no working fallback. We were forced to manually reconstruct main by stitching files together branch by branch, all in the final week before the deadline.

What Changed

We enforced a new rule: merge requests go up immediately after completing a session task — not at the end of the iteration. New branches would always start from the latest main, reducing drift. We also broke large tasks into smaller, more frequent commits to create safe checkpoints.

Frequent merges Smaller commits Branch hygiene

Was It Successful?

Yes — definitively. Iteration three produced zero merge conflicts. Our largest merge involved only 13 commits compared to 37–50 in iteration two. All ten previously-closed merge requests became a non-issue. The workflow change paid off immediately and completely.

What Went Well

Scrum meetings kept the team aligned and surfaced blockers early. Once our branching strategy improved, development flow became noticeably smoother. Scope recalibration after iteration one led to much more realistic planning in subsequent iterations.

Scrum cadence Scope calibration Iteration 3 delivery

What Went Wrong

Early overestimation of capacity led to a large gap between planned and completed dev tasks in iteration one. The merge conflict crisis in iteration two was our most costly mistake — it consumed time that should have gone to refinement and testing.

Over-scoping Merge crisis Late integration

What We Would Change If Starting Over

We would establish branching and commit conventions from day one. We would also invest more time upfront in defining system architecture so merging across layers would produce fewer conflicts. Finally, we would set lower initial velocity targets and scale up based on evidence rather than optimism.

Teamwork & Large Projects

The biggest lesson was that process matters as much as code quality. A team can write excellent individual features and still ship a broken product if integration is handled poorly. Small, frequent merges are not just a best practice — they are essential risk management on any team project with parallel workstreams.

On Iteration & Scope

Velocity charts are only useful if you act on them. Iteration one's data told us we overcommitted — and we responded by right-sizing iteration two. That feedback loop was one of the most valuable things we did as a team.

On Technical Debt

The time spent recovering from the merge conflict in iteration two is the clearest example of technical debt we encountered. Poor process choices compounded into hours of rework that could have been avoided with better habits from the start.
